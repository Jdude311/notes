1 Solving Radical Equations

1.1 Two variables, sometimes two roots

1.1.1 Steps to solve

  1. get the x's/radicals on separate sides EXAMPLE

\[\sqrt{x}=\sqrt{x+2}\]

1.1.2 When you can't isolate all radicals

  • try to isolate the more complicated radical before un-radicaling

1.1.3 No negatives under even radical

1.1.4 Answers must be plugged back in when there are positive radicals

  • Plug back in at all positions where radicals are isolated

  • Just check to make sure they stay positive or zero

    EXAMPLE

\[\sqrt{2x}-\sqrt{4x-8}=0\] \[\sqrt{2x}=\sqrt{4x-8}\] <– plug back in here \[2x=4x-8\] \[x=4\]

  • If there is something other than the radical on one side, you have to solve the whole side EXAMPLE

\[\sqrt{x}=\sqrt{2x}-2\] <– this whole side has to be solved when plugging back in

2 Roots and shit

2.1 Square Root Equations

2.1.1 Formula of square roots

\[f(x)=a\sqrt{x-h}+k\] \[(h,k)\] <– Reference point

  • a = behavior
  1. Pattern of square roots

    • Pattern

      x f(x)
      0 0
      1 1
      4 2
      9 3
      16 4
      25 5
      … …

2.1.2 Graphing square roots

  1. Example square root graph

  2. Domain and range of square roots

    \[R=[k, \infty)\] or \[R=(-\infty, k]\]

    \[D=(-\infty,h]\] or \[D=[h, \infty)\]

  3. Finding the reference point of a square root
    • When x is positive under the radical, just use the regular formula
    • When x is negative under the radical, factor out the negative like so: \[f(x)=\sqrt{3-x}+2\] \[f(x)=\sqrt{-(x-3)}+2\] \[h=3, k=2\]
  4. Using the pattern to graph square roots
    • Start at the reference point
    • Multiply the pattern of square roots by a, the behavior
    • Use the pattern to graph from the reference point as if it was the origin
    • Needs as many points as will fit on the graph

3 Glossary

3.1 Behavior

  • Determines the how compressed or stretched an equation is

3.2 Reference point

  • The point that is used as reference when graphing: (h,k)
  • Basically the vertex but on a non-quadratic equation
